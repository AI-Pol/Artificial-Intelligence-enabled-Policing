To an AI-enabled Policing Vision | January 4, 2021

An Evolution in Policing

In an AI-enabled Policing vision, Policing will be using AI to adjust their workforce. Many jobs that are high-value but human intensive will be replaced by AI-enabled counterparts, whether outright human replacements in the form of cognitive Intelligent Assistants, or as Augmented Intelligent counterparts that won’t completely replace their human peers but greatly reduce the need for human labour. An example of this could be a cognitive intelligent assistant that learns and organises information collected, driven by a frontline officer’s response to an event. These AI-enabled workforce adjustments are not just automation capabilities. However, automation is not intelligence. In fact, many of these automation tools will evolve into more intelligent process systems that can adapt and learn to Policing’s changing operational environment, automatically allocate resources as needed, and provide an intelligent console by which policing leadership can truly manage and plan their operations.

The 1st International Workshop on AI-enabled Policing will be held as one of the workshops of the EDOC 2021. The AI-ePol workshop aims at providing a forum for researchers and professionals interested in Artificial Intelligence (AI) enabled Policing Processes and Services; and in understanding, envisioning and discussing the opportunities and challenges of intelligent Process Automation, Process Data Analytics and providing Cognitive Assistants for Investigators (knowledge workers). Topic 1: Ethics of AI

The use of AI in law enforcement is associated with philosophical, contextual, and situational complexities and considerations. AI applications in law enforcement include crime pattern analysis, telephone record analysis, profiling, threat assessment, and strategic targeting of criminals. The philosophical schools of deontology and utilitarianism provide conflicting views on the integration of AI into law enforcement practices. Deontology promotes rule-based ethics, while utilitarianism advocates for actions that benefit the majority in a population. Therefore, deontologists are likely to oppose the use of AI in law enforcement due to the associated risk of cognitive privacy violations. However, utilitarianism could advocate for the application of AI to reduce crime and enhance the security for the majority of the population. Moreover, different situations and contexts determine the risk of ethics violations in AI law enforcement applications. For example, AI use in telephone record analysis and profiling correlates with an increased chance of individuals’ rights violations. However, the use of AI in crime pattern analysis and threat assessment has a limited risk of rights infringement.
